Overview

Digestion is the complex process by which the body breaks down food into nutrients, which it uses for energy, growth, and cell repair. It involves both mechanical breakdown (chewing, churning) and chemical breakdown (enzymes).

The four main stages are:

  • Ingestion: Taking in food through the mouth.
  • Digestion: Breaking down food into small organic fragments.
  • Absorption: Taking up nutrients into the bloodstream.
  • Elimination: Removing undigested waste.

Structures

Digestion begins in the oral cavity. Teeth mechanically break down food (mastication), while saliva moistens it. The pharynx serves as a passageway for both air and food.

A muscular tube that carries food from the pharynx to the stomach via rhythmic contractions called peristalsis.

A J-shaped organ that churns food with gastric juice (hydrochloric acid and enzymes) to create a semi-liquid called chyme.

Divided into the duodenum, jejunum, and ileum. Most chemical digestion and nutrient absorption occur here, aided by villi and microvilli which increase surface area.

Includes the cecum, colon, and rectum. It reabsorbs water and minerals, and processes waste for elimination.

  • Salivary Glands: Produce saliva containing enzymes.
  • Liver: Produces bile to emulsify fats.
  • Gallbladder: Stores and concentrates bile.
  • Pancreas: Produces pancreatic juice rich in enzymes and bicarbonate.

Core Functions

Mechanical Digestion

Physical breakdown of food into smaller pieces (chewing in mouth, churning in stomach, segmentation in intestines).

Chemical Digestion

Enzymatic breakdown of macromolecules into their constituent parts (monosaccharides, amino acids, fatty acids).

Absorption

Movement of nutrients from the GI tract into the blood or lymph capillaries.

Elimination

Removal of undigested materials through the anus.
